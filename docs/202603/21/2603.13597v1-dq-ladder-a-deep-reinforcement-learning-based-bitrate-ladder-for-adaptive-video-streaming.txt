# DQ-Ladder: A Deep Reinforcement Learning-based Bitrate Ladder for Adaptive Video Streaming 

Reza Farahani , Zoha Azimi , Vignesh V Menon ,Hermann Hellwagner , Radu Prodan , Schahram Dustdar and Christian Timmerer 

Abstract —Adaptive streaming of segmented video over HTTP typically relies on a predefined set of bitrate-resolution pairs, known as a bitrate ladder . However, fixed ladders often overlook variations in content and decoding complexities, leading to suboptimal trade-offs between encoding time, decoding efficiency, and video quality. This article introduces DQ-Ladder , a deep re-inforcement learning (DRL)-based scheme for constructing time-and quality-aware bitrate ladders for adaptive video streaming applications. DQ-Ladder employs predicted decoding time, qual-ity scores, and bitrate levels per segment as inputs to a Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent, guided by a weighted reward function of decoding time, video quality, and resolution smoothness. We leverage machine learning models to predict decoding time, bitrate level, and objective quality metrics (VMAF, XPSNR), eliminating the need for exhaustive encoding or quality metric computation. We evaluate DQ-Ladder using the Versatile Video Coding (VVC) toolchain (VVenC/VVdeC) on 750 video sequences across six Apple HLS-compliant resolutions and 41 quantization parameters. Experimental results against four baselines show that 

DQ-Ladder achieves BD-rate reductions of at least 10 .3 % for XPSNR compared to the HLS ladder, while reducing decoding time by 22 % . DQ-Ladder shows significantly lower sensitivity to prediction errors than competing methods, remaining robust even with up to 20 % noise. 

Index Terms —Adaptive Video Streaming, Deep Reinforcement Learning, Q-Learning, Bitrate Ladder, Quality Prediction. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Video traffic, mainly generated by video-on-demand and live streaming, dominates global Internet traffic, increasing demands for high-quality, low-latency, and sustainable con-tent delivery [1], [2]. Among streaming technologies, HTTP Adaptive Streaming (HAS), standardized by MPEG Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP (DASH) [3] and HTTP Live Streaming (HLS) [4], has become the dominant video delivery mechanism. HAS splits video content into segments, each encoded at multiple bitrates and resolutions, known as repre-sentations forming a bitrate ladder , stored on HTTP servers. HAS clients employ adaptive bitrate algorithms to select and retrieve each segment from the ladder in an appropriate representation, optimizing playback quality [5]. However, the growing demand for high-resolution content and advances in video codecs have expanded the number of representations in the ladder to accommodate diverse device capabilities [6].

Traditionally, bitrate ladder construction has relied on fixed rules that ignore content-specific characteristics affecting vi-sual quality and encoding efficiency [7]. Netflix’s per-title encoding [6] marked a shift toward content-aware encoding by analyzing each video’s rate–distortion behavior to derive improved ladders using perceptual quality metrics such as Video Multimethod Assessment Fusion (VMAF) [8]. Building on this paradigm, several recent works [9]–[11] determine resolutions within a target bitrate range to further improve compression efficiency and perceptual quality. However, these approaches typically overlook decoding time, which is in-creasingly critical for resource-constrained end-user devices. Consequently, balancing visual quality and decoding time remains a key challenge in ladder construction. Conventional heuristic-based approaches [12], [13] optimize resolution and quantization parameters (QPs) independently at each target bitrate, ignoring how early decisions influence later trade-offs such as quality consistency, decoding complexity growth, and smooth resolution transitions across the ladder [14]. In contrast, deep reinforcement learning (DRL) formulates ladder construction as a sequential decision problem, enabling a global policy that anticipates long-term quality and complexity trade-offs and coordinates decisions across the entire ladder. This article proposes DQ-Ladder , a DRL-based frame-work that balances quality, decoding time, and resolution smoothness. It exploits video complexity features and incor-porates lightweight machine learning (ML) predictors within a modular architecture to predict the expected bitrate, decoding time, and objective quality (measured via VMAF and XPSNR) for unseen video sequences. These predictions guide a Deep Q-Learning (DQN) agent to explore the multi-objective trade-off space. Extensive experiments on a dataset of 750 diverse real-world video sequences with varying content complexities demonstrate that DQ-Ladder outperforms four state-of-the-art methods, achieving BD-rate savings of at least 10 .9 % 

(PSNR) and 10 .3 % (XPSNR), while lowering decoding and encoding time by up to 22 % and 31 % . DQ-Ladder is robust to prediction errors, maintaining stable quality–decoding trade-offs even when rate–distortion and decoding-time estimates are perturbed by up to 20 % noise. The contributions of DQ-Ladder are four-fold: ( i) Modular architecture: We design a DRL-based architecture leveraging DQN for bitrate ladder construction, jointly optimizing quality and decoding complexity. ( ii) Metric prediction: We leverage lightweight ML models to predict bitrate, decoding time, and quality metrics, enabling online content-adaptive ladder gener-ation. ( iii) Problem formulation: We design a weighted reward

While Mean Opinion Score (MOS) provides the most accurate perceptual quality assessment, its cost and subjec-tivity make it impractical for real-time applications, motivat-ing learning-based approaches for MOS prediction [15]–[17]. Several works employed decision trees (DT) [18], multi-layer perceptron [19], or federated learning [20], leveraging encod-ing parameters, network conditions (delay and packet loss), and user data for MOS prediction. Beyond MOS, PSNR [21], SSIM [22], and VMAF [8] have been widely adopted for content-aware video streaming and encoding optimization. Amirpour et al. [23] present VQM4HAS, a regression model that approximates VMAF for HAS representations using low-cost features and encoder logs, avoiding explicit VMAF computation at scale. Although XPSNR seems more artifact-sensitive and computationally efficient than VMAF [24]–[26], predictive modeling remains unexplored. Kranzler et al. [27] designed a bitstream-based decoding energy prediction model using multivariate linear regression, integrated into their proposed encoder for energy-aware rate-distortion optimization. Boujida et al. [28] proposed an ML-based VVC per-frame decoding time prediction using extra trees regression (ETR) with features extracted from the bit-stream, i.e. , coding unit size, split depth, motion vectors, enabling adaptive decoding control. Ghasempour et al. [29] modeled decoding energy using video resolution and framerate as features, leveraging a lightweight lookup table approach for fast estimation in live streaming. Boujida et al. [30] extended their per-frame VVC decoding time model by integrating lightweight temporal feedback (past decoding times) and fea-ture selection, enabling accurate multi-resolution prediction for dynamic voltage and frequency scaling (DVFS)-based live streaming adaptation. Existing works rely on codec-dependent syntax or full bitstream parsing, limiting low-latency online use; their generalization to unseen content and diverse encod-ing settings remains largely unexplored. Katsenou et al. [9] provided a content-driven bitrate ladder for HEVC-encoded video sequences using regression-based prediction models. Bhat et al. [10] employed RF models to predict the best resolution for each scene, while Mueller et al. [31] combined convolutional neural networks, XGBoost, and MLP for content complexity analysis. Menon et al. [32] used random forest (RF) models to predict VMAF and select bitrate-resolution in a Just Noticeable Difference [33] (JND)-aware encoding scheme. Azimi et al. [12] integrated XGBoost models to estimate encoding time and quality metrics, enabling decoding-aware bitrate ladder construction. Lebreton et al. [7], [34] proposed ladder generation based on user quitting prob-ability to align quality with user engagement. Rajendran et al. [35] used Pareto-front analysis to predict optimized framer-ates for constructing energy-efficient ladders. Zhao et al. [36] proposed a per-shot bitrate ladder construction method that encodes a small subset of resolution-bitrate-preset points and predicts the remaining rate-distortion curves using curve fitting and ML models (e.g., RF, support vector machines, neural networks). Existing methods focus on quality alone, neglecting decoding complexity and resolution smoothness, and requiring exhaustive pre-encoding. III. DQ-L A D D E R MODEL

We design a modular architecture for DQ-Ladder to en-able efficient video segment-level bitrate ladder construction, as illustrated in Fig. 1. The architecture integrates three main modules: (1) video complexity analyzer, (2) lightweight ML-based metric predictors, and (3) a DQN agent. Given a set of video segments S = {s1, s 2, . . . , s m}, the video complexity analysis extracts spatial, temporal, and brightness features from each segment, and considers the luma-based features 

⟨EY , h, L Y ⟩. These features, combined with candidate encod-ing parameters (R, QP ), are provided to three independent predictors f1, f 2 and f3, estimating decoding time set bT ,quality set bQ, and bitrate set bB without real encoding overhead. The quality predictor is metric-agnostic. Although we train separate predictors for XPSNR and VMAF (Section VI-B), the model used at inference time is selected according to the service provider’s optimization objective. For example, when optimizing VMAF, it instantiates the VMAF predictor, and the DQN agent maximizes rewards concerning that metric. The DQN agent interacts with the environment to construct the segment-specific ladder using three main steps: 

1) DQN agent operation: The agent models the state-action value function Q(st i, a i) via a neural network and iteratively refines it using observed experience. At each decision step, for a given segment sj ∈ S and target bitrate tb i ∈ T B , the agent observes the current state st i = [ tb i, bbi−1, bqi−1, bti−1],capturing the current target bitrate tb i and the outcomes of the previous encoding step (i − 1) . This design enables fast decision-making by leveraging learned predictions, avoiding real encoding computation while retaining awareness of past encoding behavior for stability. Based on this state, the agent selects an action ai = [ ri, qp i] from the action space A,specifying the resolution ri ∈ R and quantization parameter 

qp i ∈ QP to encode the segment sj at tb i. The environment then returns the predicted metrics (bbi, bqi, bti) for the chosen configuration, used to compute the reward Rew i based on the multi-objective reward function defined in Eq. 1. To learn a policy that optimizes long-term cumulative reward, the agent minimizes the DQN loss , defined as the mean squared error between the Q-value predicted by the Q-network for the current state–action pair and a target value computed using the target network. The Q-network parameters are updated according to the Bellman optimality equation (Eq. (6)):

The operational workflow of DQ-Ladder includes two phases: (1) DQN training conducted provider-side using re-alistic encoding feedback and (2) DQN inference for ladder construction using lightweight predictions.

The agent interacts with a real encoding environment, where each seg-ment sj ∈ S is encoded with different configurations (ri, qp i)

values. At each training epoch e ∈ EP (line 4) and decision step for segment sj and target bitrate tb i ∈ T B (lines 5 -6), the agent observes the state st i = [ tb i, b i−1, q i−1, t i−1] using prior encoding results (line 7). The agent selects an action 

ai = [ ri, qp i], specifying the resolution ri and quantization parameter qp i, using an ϵ-greedy strategy (lines 8–11). With probability ϵ, a random action is chosen to explore new configurations; otherwise, with probability 1 − ϵ, the action with the highest current Q-value is selected for exploitation. The selected action triggers real encoding (line 12), from which the measured bitrate bi, quality qi, and decoding time 

ti are obtained and used to compute the reward Rew i (lines 13–14). The next state st i+1 is then observed (line 15), and the experience tuple (st i, a i, Rew i, st i+1 ) is then stored in the experience replay memory M em (line 16). Next, the Q-network parameters θ are updated using mini-batches from M em (line 17), minimizing the temporal difference loss between the predicted and target Q-values (lines 18-19). To stabilize learning, the target network parameters θ− are periodically synchronized with θ every C steps (line 20). Next, the exploration rate ϵ is linearly decayed toward ϵend (line 21) and clamped to avoid falling below ϵend (line 22). Finally, the training proceeds over subsequent epochs (line 23) until convergence. Finally, the output is the trained Q-function with parameters θ used for inference (line 24). We note that the DQN training procedure is codec-agnostic and independent of specific reward weight values. Codec-specific effects are implicitly captured by the measured bitrate, quality, and decoding time values obtained during training, while the reward weights reflect the provider’s optimization preferences. Thus, adapting DQ-Ladder to a different codec or service objective does not require changes to the learning formulation or network architecture, but only retraining with the corresponding measurements and updated reward weights.

VI. E VALUATION SETUP 

This section details the evaluation setup of DQ-Ladder .We conducted all experiments on a server with a 128 -core Intel Xeon Gold CPU and two NVIDIA Quadro GV100 GPUs. 

A. Dataset explanation and analysis 

We use 750 ultra-high-definition (UHD) video sequences from the Inter-4K dataset [37], spanning diverse con-tent types such as nature, urban, sports, and synthetic scenes. Each sequence was encoded at 60 fps using VVenC v1.11 [38] with the faster preset. As summarized in Table I, the encoding setup covers six resolutions 

R = {360 , 540 , 720 , 1080 , 1440 , 2160 }p and 41 quantization parameters QP = {10 , . . . , 50 }. For every encoded version, we recorded the actual bitrate bi, encoding time, and the de-coding time ti using VVdeC v2.3.0 [39]. Objective quality scores qi were measured using XPSNR and VMAF, providing ground-truth labels for training the DQN agent. Spatiotemporal complexity features were extracted using VCA v2.0 [40]. We analyzed the dataset characteristics to highlight the content diversity. Fig. 2 shows the distribution of video sequences in the (EY , h ) feature space, highlighting the diversity of spatial and temporal complexity, with color indicating variations in luminance LY .

B. ML-based metric prediction models 

Given the high overhead of real encoding, decoding, and quality measurements, DQ-Ladder employs lightweight ML-based predictors for decoding time, quality, and bitrate, en-abling fast ladder construction during online inference (Sec-tion V). We evaluated seven well-known ML models covering four different categories: a) Linear Regression (LR) [41] as a simple linear model; b) Random Forest (RF) [42] and Extra Trees Regression (ETR) [43] as tree-based ensemble models; 

c) AdaBoost [44], LightGBM [45], and XGBoost [46] as gradient boosting-based ensembles; d) Multi-Layer Perceptron 

(MLP) [47] as a neural network with fully connected layers. We partitioned the dataset into disjoint training ( 70 % ) and testing ( 30 % ) sets at the video level, ensuring that all segments from a given video belong exclusively to one set to avoid data leakage. Video identifiers were randomly shuffled with a fixed seed (seed = 42) to guarantee reproducibility and consistent splitting across the three prediction tasks. We applied Grid-SearchCV [48] from Scikit-learn [49] with five-fold cross-validation on the training set to search for hyperparameters in ensemble-based models. We performed an exhaustive grid search over predefined parameter spaces and retrained the best estimator on the training data. We used 10 % of the training set as a validation set and applied Optuna [50] for hyperparameter optimization in the MLP model.

While the LR model has no tunable parameters, ensemble-based models require optimization of the number of estimators ( ntrees ), maximum tree depth ( dmax ), and learning rate ( η). C. DQN agent configurations and hyperparameters 

To approximate the Q-value function, we employ a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that takes the four-dimensional state vector sit as input. All state features are normalized to the range [0 , 1] using min–max scaling to improve stability and convergence. The network consists of three connected hidden layers with 256 , 128 , and 64 neurons, using ReLU activation functions [51]. The output layer is a linear fully connected layer of size |A| , where each neuron represents the Q-value of a discrete action pair (ri, qp i). The model is implemented in PyTorch with Kaiming (He) initialization [52] and optimized using Adam [53]. The DQN agent is trained for EP = 2000 

episodes using experience replay with a memory size of 

|M em | = 10000 and a mini-batch size of BA = 128 . The target network is synchronized every C = 10 steps. We adopt an ϵ-greedy exploration strategy, with ϵ linearly decayed from 

0.8 to ϵmin = 0 .05 over 2000 steps. Hyperparameters are tuned via a combination of Optuna-based optimization [50] and man-ual refinement. The learning rate η ∈ { 0.05 , 0.005 , 0.0005 }

(depending on the experiment) and discount factor γ = 0 .7.Table III summarizes the final DQN agent hyperparameters.

3) Bitrate ladder efficiency metrics: We use the follow-ing metrics to evaluate the quality-efficiency trade-offs of the constructed bitrate ladders: a) Bjøntegaard delta rate 

(BD-rate) [54] measures the percentage bitrate increase re-quired to achieve equivalent quality (XPSNR or VMAF); lower negative BD-rate indicates higher encoding efficiency.

captures the mean absolute difference of consecutive resolu-tions; lower values indicate smoother visual transitions. 

E. Methods used for comparisons 

We compare DQ-Ladder against four baseline methods: 

a) HLS [55] uses a fixed bitrate-resolution ladder defined by Apple’s specification (Table IV). b) CDBL [12] selects the encoding configuration with the highest XPSNR, satisfying a given decoding time constraint and ensuring the decoding time remains below a threshold τL. c) RQT-PF [13] mini-mizes a composite cost function, balancing decoding time and bitrate, controlled by a trade-off parameter α, without directly optimizing video quality metrics such as XPSNR or VMAF. 

d) VEXUS [56] optimizes the bitrate ladder for bitrate-XPSNR trade-offs, maximizing quality and minimizing bitrate without considering decoding complexity. VII. E VALUATION RESULTS

Each target metric shows distinct characteristics influencing model suitability. For example, de-coding time shows regular patterns favoring tree-based models like RF. Meanwhile, bitrate and quality scores (i.e., VMAF, XPSNR) exhibit more complex, non-linear dependencies on encoding parameters, where gradient boosting and neural-based models (MLP) demonstrate higher accuracy. B. DQ-Ladder agent analysis 

We analyze the DQN agent’s behavior using four experi-ments described in this section. 

1) Reward normalization and weighting strategy: We an-alyzed the normalized distributions of decoding time and quality metrics to guide the reward function (Eq. 1) precisely. As shown in Fig. 4, even after normalization to [0 , 1] , the distributions differ significantly: decoding time is heavily right-skewed (skewness= 2.4), XPSNR is moderately right-skewed ( 0.49 ), and VMAF is slightly left-skewed ( −0.42 ) ,indicating that equal weights λ1 and λ2 cannot balance con-tributions in the reward function. Thus, we relax the constraint 

λ1 + λ2 + λ3 = 1 and allow independent tuning, enabling more flexible and data-driven trade-offs between quality and decoding efficiency.

As shown in Fig. 5 (a), the cumula-tive reward increased steadily, confirming progressive policy improvement. C. DQ-Ladder weighting coefficients analysis 

We analyze how different weighting coefficients influence 

DQ-Ladder behavior through the following experiments. 

1) Impact on bitrate ladder performance: We changed the 

λ1–λ3 values to analyze the impact of different reward weight-ings. As shown in Fig. 7, setting (1,0,0) maximizes quality (Fig. 7 (a)), improving PSNR and XPSNR by 1.26 dB and 0.96

and 2.1 s , respectively, but incur approximately 2 dB quality loss and unstable resolution shifts (Fig. 7 (d)). To balance these trade-offs, we evaluated other configurations. Among them, (0.8, 0.6, 0.1) offers the best trade-off: a BD-rate reduction of 

10 .94 % (PSNR) and 10 .3 % (XPSNR), encoding and decoding time savings of 7.96 s and 0.72 s , and improved resolution smoothness, all without degrading quality. 

2) Impact on VMAF: Starting from the initial weight configuration optimized for XPSNR, we changed the val-ues of λ1–λ3 to analyze the impact of different reward weightings on VMAF improvement. In Fig. 8 (a), configu-rations with higher λ1 and λ2, such as (0 .7, 0.8, 0.1) and 

(0 .8, 0.8, 0.1) , achieve the lowest BDR V , indicating better bitrate efficiency for VMAF. Fig. 8 (b) shows that BD-VMAF improves by approximately 5–6% with these settings, while BD-PSNR is almost identical, confirming quality gains. In terms of complexity (Fig. 8 (c)), (0 .7, 0.8, 0.1) reduces encoding and decoding times compared to (0 .8, 0.8, 0.1) ,maintaining efficiency. (λ1 = 0 .7, λ 2 = 0 .7, λ 3 = 0 .1) as the weighting configuration, yielding a BD-VMAF gain of 5 points, 36 % bitrate reduction, a BD-DeTime of −0.71 , and smooth resolution transitions, without compromising quality compared to HLS. 

D. DQ-Ladder vs. baseline analysis 

We evaluated the performance of DQ-Ladder under the selected configuration ( λ1 = 0 .8, λ 2 = 0 .6, λ 3 = 0 .1) against four state-of-the-art bitrate ladder construction schemes. 

1) Impact on bitrate ladder performance: Fig. 9 compares the average performance of DQ-Ladder against RQT-PF (with α = 0 .75 ), CDBL (with τL = 16 ), and VEXUS. VEXUS yields the highest BD-PSNR ( 1.3 dB) and BD-XPSNR (+ 1.05 

dB), but at the cost of increased encoding time ( 32 .2 s ) and decoding time ( 10 .7 s ). DQ-Ladder achieves a balance with BD-rate of −10 .94 % (PSNR) and −10 .3 % (XPSNR), while improving BD-PSNR by 0.19 dB and BD-XPSNR by 0.18 dB. 

DQ-Ladder reduces bitrate overhead and improves quality, while maintaining almost similar encoding and decoding du-rations compared to RQT-PF. DQ-Ladder also saves 6.1 s in encoding time and 0.3 s in decoding time, at a marginal bitrate cost compared to CDBL. Moreover, DQ-Ladder achieves the lowest resolution smoothness score ( 25 .5), having fewer resolution switches than all baselines. 

2) Impact on robustness to prediction errors: Threshold-based optimization methods such as CDBL derive ladder deci-sions directly from predicted decoding time and quality values, making them sensitive to estimation noise when predictions cross decision boundaries. To quantify this effect, we inject additive Gaussian noise of up to 20 % into the predicted decoding time and XPSNR metrics, following established eval-uation practice [58], [59]. As shown in Fig. 10(a–c), CDBL shows pronounced instability under noisy predictions, where its BD-rate degrades from −19 .26 % under base predictions to −2.04 % at 10 % noise and becomes positive ( 2.10 % ) at 

20 % noise. Moreover, its BD-PSNR and BD-XPSNR simulta-neously drop below zero, indicating loss of both compression efficiency and quality gains. In contrast, DQ-Ladder remains robust across all noise levels (Fig. 10(d–f)). Even with 20 % 

noise, DQ-Ladder BD-rate and BD-quality metrics remain close to the baseline setting, with only minor increases in encoding and decoding time.
