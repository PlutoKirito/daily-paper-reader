Abstract. Video object removal aims to eliminate target objects from videos while plausibly completing missing regions and preserving spatio-temporal consistency. Although diffusion models have recently advanced this task, it remains challenging to remove object-induced side effects (e.g ., shadows, reflections, and illumination changes) without compro-mising overall coherence. This limitation stems from the insufficient phys-ical and semantic understanding of the target object and its interac-tions with the scene. In this paper, we propose to introduce under-standing into erasing from two complementary perspectives. Externally, we introduce a distillation scheme that transfers the relationships be-tween objects and their induced effects from vision foundation models to video diffusion models. Internally, we propose a framewise context cross-attention mechanism that grounds each denoising block in infor-mative, unmasked context surrounding the target region. External and internal guidance jointly enable our model to understand the target ob-ject, its induced effects, and the global background context, resulting in clear and coherent object removal. Extensive experiments demon-strate our state-of-the-art performance, and we establish the first real-world benchmark for video object removal to facilitate future research and community progress. Our code, data, and models are available at: https://github.com/WeChatCV/UnderEraser. 

Keywords: Video Object Removal · Diffusion Models 

## 1 Introduction 

Video object removal aims to erase specified objects from visual content and plausibly fill the resulting regions with contextually consistent background infor-mation, while preserving spatio-temporal consistency. Previous methods, such as 3D CNN-based methods [6, 13], are limited by their constrained receptive fields, which hinder effective long-range temporal propagation. Transformer-based ap-proaches [23, 25, 44], while capable of capturing global dependencies, often in-troduce artifacts and temporal inconsistencies, particularly when input frames lack sufficient contextual information. Recent advances in diffusion models [11,32,34] have revolutionized video gen-eration and editing, with their powerful generative capabilities positioning them as promising candidates for video object removal [22, 27, 54]. However, they of-ten struggle to remove object-induced side effects ( e.g ., shadows, reflections, and illumination changes). To address this issue, ROSE [27] incorporates a mask pre-dictor built upon hidden representations, introducing additional supervision to predict the difference mask between the edited and original videos. Nevertheless, this mask predictor is largely decoupled from the video diffusion model responsi-ble for object removal. As a result, it focuses on optimizing the discriminability of the difference mask, rather than explicitly guiding the removal of objects and their associated side effects. A key limitation of existing video object removal methods is that they lack physical and semantic understanding of the target object and its interactions with the scene. To address this issue, we introduce understanding into the erasing process from two complementary perspectives: external knowledge distillation and internal condition integration. A pretrained vision foundation model naturally perceives the target object and its side effects. Leveraging this, we distill object–effect relational knowl-edge from an external vision foundation model into our video object removal diffusion model. Specifically, we locate object features and their corresponding induced side-effect features from the vision foundation model, and then model their relationships to align relational feature representations within the diffusion model. This object-induced relation distillation enables the diffusion model to capture the interactions between the target object and its induced side effects, thereby facilitating complete object removal. The nearby background provides the most informative visual cues for ob-ject removal. To help the model interpret the masked region in relation to its background, we inject background information as internal conditioning through cross-attention, a natural conditioning mechanism in diffusion models. While standard cross-attention produces a single embedding for an entire video, we modify it to operate at the frame level for more precise guidance. With our 

framewise context cross-attention , the model achieves more faithful con-tent synthesis and improved spatio-temporal consistency. Benefiting from comprehensive external and internal guidance, our model can clearly remove the induced effects of the target object, as shown in Fig. 1. Moreover, object removal in long videos often suffers from temporal inconsis-tency due to a train–test mismatch in frame numbers. To address this, we intro-duce keyframe-guided propagation, substantially improving consistency in long videos. Furthermore, there is no real-world benchmark for video object removal. To enable thorough experimental evaluation and facilitate future research, we establish the first real-captured dataset with manually refined masks and ground-truth annotations. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our model outper-forms state-of-the-art methods across fidelity, coherence, and motion consistency. The key contributions of our work are as follows: 

– We present a powerful video object removal model with external and inter-nal understanding guidance. It provides physical and semantic awareness of target objects and their side effects, enabling clear and coherent removal. 

– We introduce external object-induced relation distillation from a pretrained vision foundation model and internal framewise context cross-attention to in-ject informative background condition. Working in tandem, these two strate-gies enhance content completion and spatio-temporal consistency. 

– Extensive experiments demonstrate our superior performance. We also es-tablish the first real-captured benchmark and propose a keyframe-guided propagation scheme for long video object removal. Our code, model, and benchmark will be publicly released to support future research and advance the community. 

## 2 Related Work 

Video Diffusion Models. Recent diffusion-based approaches have made rapid progress in generating videos conditioned on text or images [3, 4, 7, 9, 10, 33]. 4 D. Liu et al. 

Among these methods, AnimateDiff [9] adapts pretrained text-to-image diffusion models for video synthesis by introducing dedicated motion components. Imagen Video [10] and Make-a-Video [33] learn spatiotemporal generation more directly by training cascaded spatial and temporal modules in pixel space. These works typically instantiate the denoising network using a U-Net backbone. Diffusion Transformer (DiT) [29] has become the prevailing paradigm for video generation. Representative systems such as Sora [5], CogVideoX [41], Hun-yuanVideo [17], and StepVideo [26] combine DiT with large-scale training and improved latent video compression to enhance temporal coherence and synthesis quality. Wan [36] further advances this line of work by introducing a scalable spatiotemporal VAE. These models illustrate the growing effectiveness and versa-tility of DiT-based frameworks for high-resolution, long-duration, and physically plausible video synthesis. 

Video Inpainting. Video inpainting aims to restore masked or missing regions in a video by synthesizing content that is spatially coherent with the surrounding context and temporally consistent across frames. Early works employed 3D CNNs [6, 13, 37] to capture spatiotemporal fea-tures but were limited by small receptive fields and frame misalignment, re-ducing their ability to utilize distant context. Flow-based pixel propagation ap-proaches [8,16,40,45,47] effectively recover fine textures and details by leveraging neighboring frames. Transformer-based methods [20,21,23,25,44,46,53] leverage spatiotemporal attention to capture recurrent textures and contextual informa-tion across frames, enabling more effective reconstruction of object structures and coherent content in missing regions. Specifically, ProPainter [53] integrates recurrent flow completion, dual-domain propagation, and a mask-guided sparse Transformer to propagate known pixels and partially generate missing regions. In recent years, diffusion-based methods [2, 14, 18, 19, 22, 27, 39, 51, 55] have become mainstream due to their superior ability to capture complex data distri-butions and their more stable performance. Among these, DiffuEraser [22] adapts image inpainting model BrushNet [15] for video inpainting through a two-stage training strategy. MiniMax-Remover [54] introduces a two-stage diffusion-based video object removal framework that eliminates textual conditioning and em-ploys minimax distillation to achieve high-quality removal. ROSE [27], built on the Wan2.1 model [36], utilizes a diffusion transformer and synthetic paired data to remove objects together with their associated side effects. Despite the development of video inpainting, state-of-the-art models still struggle to completely remove the target object along with its induced effects. In this paper, we address this problem by introducing external and internal understanding guidance.

3.2 Object-Induced Relation Distillation 

Several works [35, 52] have shown that a pretrained diffusion model for visual generation possesses visual perception capabilities. However, we observed that for understanding objects with their induced effects, which requires a deeper understanding of physics and structure, diffusion models are far from sufficient. In Fig. 3, we visualize the attention between a pretrained Vision Foundation Model (VFM) and Video Diffusion Model (VDM). VFM attention is sharply concentrated not only on the target object but also on its induced side effects and their spatial/semantic relations. In contrast, the responses of the VDM are much weaker and poorly localized around these object-induced effects, suggesting limited sensitivity to the causal cues required for reliable side-effect removal. This observation aligns with the intrinsic differences between VFM and VDM. Trained on large-scale visual data, VFM learns object-centric representations and implicitly encodes correlations between objects and their induced effects. VDM, while strong in synthesis, may lack such fine-grained object–effect under-standing, which can lead to incomplete or inconsistent removal of side effects. 6 D. Liu et al. Discussion. Our OIRD significantly differs from generic understanding-to-gene-ration representation alignment methods [42,50], which align all features globally. In contrast, OIRD focuses on the removal of object side effects by distilling only the cross-region relationships between the target object and its induced side effects. Additionally, from an alignment design perspective, OIRD offers a softer and more stable regularization for fine-tuning pretrained VDM by matching relational similarities, rather than enforcing strict feature alignment as in [42] or global spatiotemporal matching as in [50]. 

3.3 Framewise Context Cross-Attention 

The nearby background provides crucial visual cues for object removal, offering reliable information to restore missing regions and helping the generator un-derstand the masked area in context. Naturally, we propose using background 8 D. Liu et al. 

information as internal conditioning during denoising, enabling the model to bet-ter interpret the scene, reduce hallucination and drift, and improve the stability and consistency of the inpainted results. A key question is how to effectively inject such contextual guidance into the generator. Rather than introducing extra networks or specialized adapters, we leverage the native cross-attention blocks in video diffusion models to condi-tion the denoising process on complementary-region cues. While video diffusion models naturally include cross-attention blocks to fuse textual and visual fea-tures, their potential for object removal has not been fully explored: ROSE [27] passes an empty text prompt, whereas MiniMax-Remover [54] removes the cross-attention blocks entirely. In contrast, we preserve the original architecture and activate cross-attention using visual content outside the target object regions. Specifically, we feed the model with visual features extracted from the un-masked regions via a CLIP [30] vision encoder. Formally, this guidance is repre-sented as

(1 − Mobj ) ensures that only the unmasked background contributes to the cross-attention guidance, steering the model to reconstruct the removed object region from its surrounding context. A practical challenge is that the cross-attention in video diffusion models is typically designed to use a single condition to guide all frames jointly, whereas in video object removal, each frame has a distinct context. Thus, we modify cross-attention into a framewise mode. To implement this without altering the original architecture, we adopt a simple reshaping strategy. Given background visual tokens with shape [B × (F × H × W ) × C], we merge the batch and frame dimensions and reshape them into [( B × F ) × (H × W ) × C]. This operation treats each frame as an independent “sample” in the batch, allowing us to apply the traditional cross-attention layer to inject unmasked background context for each frame. Finally, we reshape the tokens back to their original shape. 

3.4 Keyframe-Guided Propagation for Long Videos 

We observe that object removal on long videos generally produces unstable re-sults. This limitation stems from the model being trained only on fixed-length clips, limiting its ability to generalize to longer video sequences. Training. For each training clip, we randomly sample a temporal stride n ∈{2, . . . , 10 } and treat every n-th frame as a keyframe. 4.3 Ablation Studies Object-Induced Relation Distillation (OIRD). As shown in Table 3, OIRD consistently improves reconstruction quality, achieving higher PSNR/SSIM and lower LPIPS than the baseline. It also enables more complete and coherent removal of objects and their induced side effects (Fig. 9), demonstrating the effectiveness of transferring object–side-effect relations from a VFM. 

Framewise Context Cross-Attention (FCCA). Building on OIRD, en-abling FCCA further improves all metrics, achieving the best performance in Table 3. By grounding each denoising block in informative unmasked background context, FCCA complements the distilled relational understanding and provides more reliable cues for completing uncertain masked regions. This leads to more plausible inpainting and improved spatio-temporal consistency (Fig. 9). 

Different alignment depth. We further examine the effect of where to impose VFM-guided alignment by aligning the outputs of different VDM blocks to VFM features. Fig. 10 shows that using the 15-th block as the alignment point gives the best performance. Correspondingly, we set Lh in Fig. 2 to 15 for our model. 

Effect of λ. We try different values of λ for the weight of LOIRD loss. Fig. 11 shows that λ = 0 .1 provides the best trade-off between the original diffusion loss and the proposed LOIRD loss. Correspondingly, we set λ = 0 .1 for our model. 

4.4 Long Videos 

We show results on videos with more than 290 frames, which is much higher than the training frame number of 81. Without the proposed Keyframe-Guided Propagation (KGP), the model suffers from weird color artifacts (the left sample in Fig. 12) and temporal flickering (the right sample in Fig. 12). With KGP, the model avoids color bias and yields more stable results for long-video processing. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose a framework for video object removal that improves diffusion models by introducing stronger object understanding from both exter-From Understanding to Erasing 15 

nal and internal perspectives. We transfer object–effect relationships from vision foundation models through a distillation scheme and design a framewise con-text cross-attention mechanism to ground denoising in informative surrounding context. Together, these components enable more effective removal of objects and their induced side effects while maintaining spatio-temporal coherence. Ex-tensive quantitative and qualitative experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art performance. In addition, we introduce the first real-world benchmark for video object removal to support future research, as well as an effective solution for long videos. We believe our work will benefit the development of video object removal and the broader research community.
