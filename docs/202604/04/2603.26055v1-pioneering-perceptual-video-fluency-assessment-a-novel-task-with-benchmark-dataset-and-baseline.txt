## Abstract 

Accurately estimating humans’ subjective feedback on 

video fluency , e.g ., motion consistency and frame continu-ity, is crucial for various applications like streaming and gaming. Yet, it has long been overlooked, as prior arts have focused on solving it in the video quality assessment (VQA) task, merely as a sub-dimension of overall quality. In this work, we conduct pilot experiments and reveal that current VQA predictions largely underrepresent fluency, thereby limiting their applicability. To this end, we pioneer Video Fluency Assessment (VFA) as a standalone perceptual task focused on the temporal dimension. To advance VFA re-search, 1) we construct a fluency-oriented dataset, FluVid, comprising 4,606 in-the-wild videos with balanced fluency distribution, featuring the first-ever scoring criteria and hu-man study for VFA. 2) We develop a large-scale benchmark of 23 methods, the most comprehensive one thus far on FluVid, gathering insights for VFA-tailored model designs. 

3) We propose a baseline model called FluNet, which de-ploys temporal permuted self-attention (T-PSA) to enrich input fluency information and enhance long-range inter-frame interactions. Our work not only achieves state-of-the-art performance but, more importantly, offers the com-munity a roadmap to explore solutions for VFA. Both Flu-Vid and FluNet will be available at https://github. com/KeiChiTse/VFA .

Video, as a representative multimedia medium, has experi-enced rapid growth in recent years, playing a pivotal role across various domains, such as social media [4], content creation [23], and live e-commerce [54]. According to Cisco’s Visual Networking Index (VNI) [3], global IP video traffic is expected to account for more than 82% of all IP traffic by 2022. Meanwhile, as video-capturing and view-ing devices advance, users increasingly prefer videos that appear to be of “high quality” [9]. To tackle this challenge, the prevailing approach is to uti-lize off-the-shelf video quality assessment (VQA) models to quantify quality scores, thereby automatically emulating humans’ subjective feedback on video quality [26, 53, 76, 79]. As VQA research progresses [1, 67, 85], numerous

Second , VFA prediction can enhance text/image-to-video generation by serving as a fluency as-sessment metric or a reward for reinforcement learning. In this paper, we dissect the proposed task and pinpoint three problems to advance VFA research: First , there is a lack of a human subjective rating standard for video flu-ency. As proved in VQA literature, a robust scoring stan-dard—such as ITU-R BT.500 [43]—serves as the corner-stone for defining subjective fluency levels and compar-ing the performance of different assessors [18, 50]. Sec-ond , the lack of large-scale human studies and datasets with ground-truth fluency scores y presents a challenge, which not only hinders the benchmarking of various approaches but also constrains the full potential of data-driven methods empowered by deep learning [70, 86]. Last , current VQA approaches lack explicit model designs to capture fluency, making them inadequate as baselines for the VFA task. 

To address the 1st problem , we formulate a five-tier accurate category rating (ACR) criteria to mapping human fluency perception ( e.g ., “good”) to numerical scores ( e.g ., 4.0), and vice versa. The definition of five fluency levels is written by a group of 20 visual experts, strictly follow-ing the instructions in the ITU regulations [44, 45] for cor-rectness. To address the 2nd problem , we conduct the first-ever human study for the VFA task using the proposed criteria, thereby constructing a dataset termed FluVid for performance benchmarking. FluVid comprises over 4,000 videos exhibiting diverse levels of fluency, each accompa-nied by a Mean Opinion Score (MOS) annotated by 20 ex-perts. Moreover, to mitigate video selection bias and ad-dress the long-tailed fluency distribution for robust bench-marking, we meticulously devise a set of principles to guide both video collection and annotation. To address the 3rd problem , through a careful analysis of the performance of 23 models on FluVid, we ascertain that limited input frames and insufficient inter-frame interactions are the ob-stacles preventing them from perceiving fluency. Therefore, we propose a simple yet powerful baseline model named 

FluNet . FluNet overcomes the obstacles by a computation-ally efficient attention mechanism to compress and permute the channels of K, V matrices. Moreover, to address the reliance on expert-annotated fluency scores and leave Flu-Vid for benchmarking purposes, we devise a self-supervised (SSL) training strategy . By synthesizing and learning to rank videos with various fluency levels, FluNet surpasses previous methods in VFA by a notable margin in a data-efficient manner. Our main contributions are four-fold: • We pioneer an investigation into the task paradigm of VQA, revealing that current VQA models fall short in quantifying temporal quality. To address this, we formu-late temporal quality as fluency and present a novel per-ceptual task —Video Fluency Assessment (VFA). • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to estab-lish the subjective fluency scoring standard and conduct a large-scale human study to construct the dataset FluVid ,covering over 4,000 videos and fluency-centred MOSs for performance benchmarking. • We conduct a comprehensive evaluation on FluVid, cov-ering 6 VQA methods and 17 open-sourced large multi-modal models (LMMs) to measure their effectiveness in understanding video fluency. The results expose notable deficiencies in current methods and provide critical in-sights for future improvement. • Driven by the analysis of benchmark results, we propose the baseline model FluNet along with a ranking-based 

training strategy . Extensive experiments and ablation studies prove the effectiveness of our method. 

## 2. Related Work 

Tasks. Accurately assessing perceptual fluency plays a crit-ical role in multiple video tasks, including video quality assessment (VQA), video frame interpolation (VFI), and video stabilization (VS). First , VQA aims to evaluate the overall quality to mimic humans’ feedback [70, 80]. How-ever, the predicted scores fail to disentangle spatial and tem-poral quality, and cannot serve as reliable indicators of flu-ency (as in Tab.1). In contrast, our proposed VFA yields ac-curate indicators for fluency. Second , VFI aims to generate intermediate frames between known frames to enhance mo-tion coherence [82, 88]. Assessing the change in fluency be-fore and after interpolation is crucial. Currently, VFI relies on pixel-based metrics, such as PSNR [42] and SSIM [62], or VQA-based metrics, like LPIPS [83] and NIQE [38]. Un-like these metrics, our proposed VFA method aligns well with HVS. Last , VS aims to eliminate unintended camera shakes to produce stable camera motion [61, 87]. Assessing the fluency of camera motion before and after stabilization is crucial. Currently, VS estimates a stability score by com-puting the differential of the camera trajectory [78]. How-ever, this score struggles to capture subtle changes in flu-ency [36], and our proposed VFA method can address this limitation for VS. 

Datasets. Numerous efforts in perceptual tasks ( e.g ., VQA) rely on time-consuming crowdsourcing to construct large-scale datasets of human subjective scores ( e.g ., MOS), thereby enabling the training and benchmarking of mod-els [7, 18, 58]. The data construction involves two steps: 1) video collection and 2) instructing annotators to score the videos by established criteria [43]. Recent VQA datasets exhibit features such as greater content diversity [67], larger scale [76], and more annotation modalities [11]. However, the above datasets focus on holistic video quality, lacking fluency-centered video collections and fine-grained annota-tions. Therefore, they are not suitable for the VFA task and cannot support the generalization of VFA models. To ad-dress this, we introduce FluVid with the first fine-grained human study on fluency. 

Methods. In the early stages, objective metrics such as stuttering rate and jank number are used to represent video fluency [8, 63]. However, they cannot align with hu-man subjective feedback [6]. Concurrently, cognitive sci-ence research seeks to model the HVS perception of mo-tion for fluency assessment [22, 24]; however, these ap-proaches overly simplify neural mechanisms and overlook individual variability [48]. In recent years, data-driven approaches have driven remarkable progress in perceptual tasks (e.g., VQA), with a focus on generating more precise scores [26, 53, 65, 84] or leveraging LMMs to produce lin-guistic descriptions [69, 71, 77]. Nevertheless, these meth-ods typically extract holistic quality features and lack tai-lored designs for perceiving subtle fluency variations, re-sulting in poor performance (as in Tab.1). To address this, we propose FluNet to yield numerical scores that accurately reflect perceptual fluency. 

## 3. The Benchmark Dataset: FluVid 

Given the lack of quantitative evaluation, it is imperative to establish a benchmark for the VFA task. This section elaborates on the construction pipeline and analysis of the first-ever VFA benchmark termed FluVid as follows. 

3.1. Construction Principles 

To avoid video selection bias and alleviate long-tailed flu-ency distribution for reliable benchmarking, the FluVid is designed based on two guiding principles (Fig.2 (a)-(b)): • We specify three video components that dominate fluency and ensure the collected videos cover all of them (Fig.2 (a)). Since interrelated factors ( e.g ., unstable shooting, dramatic light changes, etc .) affect video fluency, it is impractical to enumerate all factors and gather sufficient videos for each. Inspired by visual psychology and cog-nitive science research [15, 35, 37], we discover that all factors are governed by three video components: the fore-ground , the background , and the camera . Therefore, we collect three categories of videos, each with its flu-ency primarily determined by a specific video component. This principle simplifies the collection while enriching di-versity, allowing for a deeper understanding of VFA. • We assure the diversity of video content and parameters 

(Fig.2 (b)). Recent VQA advancements [26, 65] suggest that fluency is related to high-level semantics . More-over, perceptual fluency is affected by parameters such as frame rate [59, 60]. Therefore, FluVid collects videos with varied content categories and parameters to avoid selection bias. 

3.2. Video Curation 

Source selection. As in Fig.2(c), we select the action dataset SSv2 [16] as one of the sources, as it comprises videos in which the foreground movements , such as the hu-man body or objects, affect perceptual fluency (following 

principle 1 ). Given its considerable size (over 220,000 videos), we randomly sample 100 videos each from 174 action classes, obtaining 17,400 videos. In addition, we incorporate five UGC-VQA datasets as sources, including LSVQ [76], KonViD-1k [18], LIVE-VQC [50], YouTube-UGC [59], and Maxwell [67]. These datasets feature videos in which perceptual fluency stems from background changes or camera trajectories (following principle 1 ) and cover various parameters , including resolution, duration, shooting devices, and shooting conditions (following prin-ciple 2 ). We sample from the UGC-VQA datasets and dedu-plicate to get 45,000 unique videos. To this end, a total of 624,000 videos are collected for the subsequent filtering. 

Filtering. As in Fig.2(c), a filtering process is applied to ensure content and parameter diversity (following princi-ple 2 ). Specifically, 10 distinct scene categories are se-lected from six source datasets. We leverage an off-the-shelf contrastive video captioner, ViFi-CLIP [41], to ef-ficiently compute the similarity between each video and the 10 scene types, acquiring the content distribution of all videos. A uniform sampling is conducted on 624,000 videos to guarantee content and parameter diversity, yield-ing 7,648 videos. In addition, numerous works indicate that 

severe spatial distortions impair human judgment of video fluency [39, 74]. We exclude videos exhibiting such distor-tions from 7,648 videos to prevent a long-tailed fluency dis-tribution. Specifically, we remove UGC-VQA videos with excessively low MOS and employ Fast-VQA [64] to predict the quality scores of videos from SSv2, discarding those with scores below 2.0, yielding 4,606 videos after filtering.

## 4. The Baseline Model: FluNet 

A potential reason that hinders VQA methods from perceiv-ing fluency is the limited temporal information in the sparse frames ( e.g ., 16 frames). Previous studies suggest that a di-rect solution is to increase the number of input frames or the frame rate [13, 49, 52]. Inspired by [90], we propose 

temporal permuted self-attention (T-PSA) to accommo-date the increased input frames and inter-frame interac-tions , while balancing computation simultaneously. Given T-PSA, we construct a powerful yet simple baseline model ,termed FluNet . Moreover, we propose an SSL training strategy to enhance FluNet’s fluency perception capability and overcome the scarcity of fluency scores, described next.

## 5. Experiments 

23 deep learning-based methods are first benchmarked on FluVid to evaluate their performance. One state-of-the-art method is then retrained and compared with FluNet. Exten-sive ablations are last conducted to prove FluNet’s efficacy. 

5.1. Benchmark on FluVid 

Model participants. For VQA methods , we evaluate six models with two variants, including Fast-VQA [64], Faster-VQA [66], DOVER [65], PVQ [76], and SimpleVQA [53]. For LMMs, we evaluate nine video LMMs , including Video-LLaVA [28], Chat-UniVi-v1&v1.5 [20], LLaMA-VID [56], Video-ChatGPT [34], PLLaVA [72], Qwen 2.5-VL [2], VQA 2-Scorer [19], and FineVQ [11], as well as eight im-age LMMs with four variants, comprising ShareGPT4V [5], LLaVA-OneVision [25], LLaVA-v1.5 [29], Q-Instruct [69], and Q-Align [68]. 

Evaluation settings. Our proposed VFA task adopts the SRCC and PLCC as the metrics. Both metrics range in [-1, 1]; a larger SRCC indicates a better ranking between sam-ples, and a larger PLCC shows a more accurate prediction. 5.2. Compare FluNet with State-of-the-Art 

Implementation details. During training and inference, FluVid takes a clip of 128 continuous frames, and each is re-sized to 224 ×224. For T-PSA, the window size is (32 , 7, 7) ,channel dimension C is 96, and γ is 2. The four stages of the encoder Fe have (2 , 2, 6, 2) blocks, respectively. Fe is initialized by Swin-T [32] pre-trained on Kinetics-400 [21]. For rank learning and fine-tuning, experiments are con-ducted on eight NVIDIA V100 GPUs. The batch size is 16. AdamW is adopted with a weight decay of 0.01 and 0.05, and training lasts for 30 and 60 epochs, respectively. The learning rates are 3e-4 and 1e-5, respectively, and decayed by cosine annealing without warmup.

5.3. Ablation Studies on FluNet 

To validate the effectiveness of FluNet and analyze the core design choices, ablation studies are conducted as follows: 

Effectiveness of T-PSA. In Tab.5, we discuss the effective-ness of T-PSA. The 1st row represents the vanilla video Swin-T, which takes a maximum of 64 frames using a win-dow size of (32 , 7, 7) . With the same window size, T-PSA introduces channel compression to reduce GFLOPs. As T-PSA is implemented in more blocks ( 2nd -5th rows), we find that the model processes additional frames, re-sulting in continuous performance improvements. More-over, with compressed channels of K and V, T-PSA shows greater parameter efficiency compared to vanilla window self-attention. 

Impact of window size in T-PSA. The impact of window size is studied in Tab.6. The result in the 1st row indicates that, although increased input frames encompass rich flu-ency information, a short temporal side length, as adopted by Fast-VQA, fails to model the overall fluency variations in the entire video. Instead, we believe it concentrates on the divergences between local frames. Conversely, results in 2nd -3rd rows underscore the importance of simultane-ously increasing both the window size and the number of input frames for improved understanding of fluency. 

Effectiveness of training strategy. The ablation of the training strategy is performed in Tab.7. Comparing the 

5th row with the 1st -3rd rows, a clear conclusion can be drawn: both rank learning and fine-tuning boost fluency as-sessment. Further, we compare the 4th row with the joint training scheme ( 3rd row), which uses 606 labeled videos to synthesize ranked videos. The training loss is a weighted sum of Lrank and Lft : Lft + αL rank , where α = 0 .3. Al-though joint training outperforms the results in the 1st and 

2nd rows, the advantage of two-stage training lies in its ability to leverage massive unsupervised videos during rank learning. We believe the true potential of joint training will be revealed as the volume of fluency annotation continues to grow. 

Impact of synthesized fluency levels. In Tab.8, the effect of the number of synthesized fluency levels, K, used in rank learning is evaluated. A larger K allows for a more fine-grained fluency assessment, making the ranking task more challenging. The best result can be acquired when K is 7. 

## 6. Concluding Remarks and Future Work 

In this paper, we present a comprehensive study on the newly proposed VFA task by developing a baseline model, constructing a fluency-oriented benchmark dataset, and es-tablishing the largest-scale benchmark to date. Extensive experiments and analyses underscore the significance of the VFA task. As a pioneering effort, future directions include scaling up fluency annotations, defining more fine-grained annotation criteria, and exploring more sophisticated model architectures. Overall, we hope this work will inspire the community to reevaluate current paradigms in perceptual assessment and contribute to the advancement of QoE. References

## 1. More Details of FluVid 

1.1. Scoring Criteria 

As described in the method section of the manuscript, we design a novel five-point Absolute Category Rating (ACR) standard, meticulously adhering to the ITU regulations [42– 45], designed to evaluate the perceptual fluency of 4,606 candidate videos. It emphasizes the continuity of video across the temporal dimension, such as the stability of frame rates and the consistency of motion. 1.2. Annotation Process 

In the human study section, we describe the annotation pro-cess of FluVid. Here, we provide the annotation GUI as in Fig.1. On the left side, annotators need to watch the video playback thoroughly, focusing on the fluency of fore-ground objects, background, and camera movement. View-ers are allowed to watch the playback repeatedly. At the same time, annotators need to give their fluency rating on the right panel, following the criteria in Tab.1, as all of them are trained by scoring 100 anchor videos. We collect the fi-nal scores of 20 annotators on each video and average them for the final results. 

1.3. Comparison to Existing Perceptual Datasets 

In the related work and method section, we highlight that FluVid is the first-ever perceptual benchmark dataset fo-cused on fluency. Here, we provide a comprehensive com-parison among FluVid and other perceptual datasets (mostly in VQA task) in Tab.2. We discover that most VQA datasets only provide holistic video quality scores ( e.g ., LIVE-VQC [47], KoNViD-1k [18] etc .). While some datasets provide scores on one or more quality dimensions, such as DOVER [65] and LSVQ [76]. They do not disentan-gle fluency and pay insufficient attention to the temporal dimension. Additionally, though Maxwell [67] provides scores partly reflecting temporal quality (T-8), its scoring criteria are only a three-tier system, including a ternary choice of “bad”, “neutral”, and “good”. This three-tier sys-tem is not fine-grained enough to align with the human visual system (HVS) and has not been proven by a large-scale human study to be as robust as our proposed five-tier rating. FineVQ [11] constructs a VQA database FineVD, which includes temporal quality scores besides overall qual-ity scores. However, it focuses on addressing the VQA task, treating fluency merely as a sub-dimension of overall qual-ity. Conversely, we treat fluency as the sole focus of the newly proposed VFA. Moreover, we conduct an in-depth analysis of fluency and dissect its factors into three video components, as in the method section. Last but not least, the research focus of FineVD is on the language instruc-tions for large multimodal models (LMMs). 

## 2. More Details of FluNet 

2.1. Limitations of Synthesis and Mitigation 

In the method section, we emphasize that fluency is very complicated, sometimes dominated by foreground, back-ground, or camera. Still, we only drop and duplicate the whole frame when synthsizing stuttered videos, as in Fig.5 and Alg.1 of our paper. A potential limitation for this “drop-and-duplicate” strategy is that the synthesized stutter can-not cover the complex real-world scenarios. To mitigate this, we further fine-tune FluNet on 606 videos with accu-rate fluency scores to enhance its generalization ability. In future work, we will 1) introduce more sophisticated syn-thesis, such as applying the video segmentation techniques to change the fluency level of foreground, background, and camera, respectively. 2) Scale up FluVid to enable more videos for supervised training.

## 3. More Details of Experiments 

3.1. Rationale of Model Participant Selection 

In the experimental settings section, we briefly introduce 23 model participants. Here, we explain the rationale be-hind our selection. First, we select VQA methods based on their performance on common VQA benchmarks, including LSVQ, LIVE-VQC, KoNViD-1k and YouTube-UGC [59]. Thus, six state-of-the-art VQA methods are selected. Sec-ond, large multimodal models (LMMs) begin to excel in multiple vision tasks, including high-level ( e.g ., classifi-cation, detection etc .) and low-level tasks ( e.g ., deblur-ring). Therefore, it is imperative to benchmark them on our VFA task. Among them, video LMMs are selected due to their superior ability to process videos. Specifi-cally, we choose seven video LMMs with best performance on general video benchmarks, including VideoMME [14], MMBench-Video [12], MVBench [27], and MLVU [89]. We also choose two video LMMs specialized in VQA, including FineVQ and VQA 2-Scorer. Recent literature suggests image LMMs exhibit primary quality perception ability. Hence, we select three image LMMs with top-tier performance on general image benchmarks, including MME [75], MMBench [30], MMMU [81], MTVQA [55], and two image LMMs specialized in image quality assess-ment (IQA).
