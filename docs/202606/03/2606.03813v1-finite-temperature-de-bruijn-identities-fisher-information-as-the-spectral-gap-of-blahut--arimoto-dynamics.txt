7.2 What This Discovery Explains 

The identification Œª‚àó = J/ (2 Œ≤) provides clear answers to the three questions raised in the introduction: 1. Why Fisher information? Because it is the spectral gap of the rate-distortion Hessian. The appearance of J in de Bruijn‚Äôs identity is not an algebraic accident of integration by parts‚Äîit reflects the geometry of optimal reconstruction. Specifically, the relaxation kernel G is the Fisher‚ÄìRao Hessian of the BA free energy (Theorem 2.2), and its smallest eigenvalue Œª‚àó measures the local curvature of the free energy landscape. Theorem 3.1 identifies this curvature as the regularized Fisher information. 2. Why the factor 1/2? Because Œª‚àó = J/ (2 Œ≤), and the finite-temperature identity Œ≤ ‚àÇF Œ≤ /‚àÇœÉ 2 =1/(2 œÉ2) carries this factor exactly. The factor 1/2 is not an artifact of the heat equation proof; it originates from the relationship between the spectral gap and the source variance in the Gaussian BA fixed point. 3. Why Gaussian sources are special? Because for Gaussian sources, the BA relaxation kernel G is exactly diagonalized by Hermite polynomials, and the translation mode (score function ‚àÇ log p) is an exact eigenvector of G with eigenvalue Œª‚àó. For non-Gaussian sources, 15 the translation mode is only an approximate eigenvector, and the equality Œª‚àó = J/ (2 Œ≤)

becomes the variational bound Œª‚àó ‚â§ ‚ü® v, Gv‚ü©‚àó. This extremal property of Gaussian sources is a spectral analogue of the well-known fact that Gaussians maximize entropy under variance constraints and minimize Fisher information under the same. 

7.3 Implications for the Entropy Power Inequality 

The classical proof of the entropy power inequality (EPI) [3, 8] uses de Bruijn‚Äôs identity as a crucial lemma. Our finite-temperature extension suggests the possibility of a finite-temperature EPI formulated in terms of BA free energies, which would reduce to the classical EPI in the zero-temperature limit. Specifically, the œá2-dissipation identity and the spectral gap‚ÄìFisher relation provide a vari-ational structure that may yield a direct proof of the EPI entirely within the BA framework, complementing the classical heat-equation approach. If one can establish a tensorization prop-erty for the relaxation kernel under convolution‚Äîanalogous to how Fisher information tensorizes: 

J(X + Y ) ‚â§ J(X) + J(Y )‚Äî then the dissipation structure of BA dynamics would directly imply the EPI. We leave this exciting direction for future work. 

7.4 Open Problems 

The results of this paper open several avenues for further investigation: 1. Tensorization of the relaxation kernel. Under convolution of independent sources, how does the relaxation kernel G behave? Does it satisfy a subadditivity property analo-gous to that of Fisher information? A positive answer would enable a BA-spectral proof of the entropy power inequality. 2. Wasserstein geometry. The BA free energy is closely related to entropic optimal trans-port [7]. Does the spectral gap Œª‚àó admit an interpretation in the Wasserstein geometry of the source distribution? In particular, the translation mode used in the variational bound (Theorem 5.3) is the infinitesimal generator of spatial translations, suggesting a connection to Otto calculus on the Wasserstein space. 3. Algorithmic implications. The identity Œª‚àó = J/ (2 Œ≤) predicts that BA dynamics con-verges faster for sources with larger Fisher information. This suggests the design of adap-tive temperature schedules for BA algorithms based on local estimates of J(p), potentially accelerating convergence for multi-modal or heavy-tailed sources. 4. Exact finite-temperature identity for general sources. Can the variational inequal-ity Œª‚àó ‚â§ ‚ü® v, Gv‚ü©‚àó be upgraded to an exact identity for general sources by identifying the ap-propriate correction term? For Gaussian sources, the correction vanishes because the score function is an exact eigenfunction of G. Characterizing this correction for non-Gaussian sources would provide a deeper understanding of the spectral geometry of rate-distortion optimization. 

7.5 Conclusion 

Classical de Bruijn‚Äôs identity has stood since 1959 as a foundational link between differential entropy and Fisher information. This paper shows that it is not an isolated fact, but the zero-temperature shadow of a richer structure living in the spectral geometry of rate-distortion optimization. The key that unlocks this structure is the identification that the BA spectral gap is the regularized Fisher information: Œª‚àó = J/ (2 Œ≤). This identification connects two major branches of information theory‚Äî rate-distortion theory and information inequalities‚Äîthat have developed 16 largely independently, and opens new directions for the geometric study of information-theoretic inequalities. The finite-temperature de Bruijn identity, ‚àÇF Œ≤ /‚àÇœÉ 2 = Œª‚àó, is both simpler and more funda-mental than its classical counterpart: it holds exactly for all temperatures, requires no limiting procedure, and reveals Fisher information as the spectral gap of the rate-distortion Hessian. Classical de Bruijn follows as the elementary consequence Œ≤ ‚àÇF Œ≤ /‚àÇœÉ 2 = J/ 2, valid at every finite Œ≤.We hope that this thermal perspective on de Bruijn‚Äôs identity will stimulate further investi-gations into the spectral geometry of information theory.
